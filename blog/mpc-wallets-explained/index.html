MPC Wallets Explained: Why Enterprises Are Ditching Hardware Wallets

Nov 20, 20248 min read
← Back to Blog

Multi-Party Computation (MPC) wallets are replacing hardware wallets and multi-sig setups as the institutional standard for crypto custody. Here's why.

What is MPC?

MPC splits a private key into multiple shares distributed across different parties or devices. No single party ever holds the complete key. Transactions require a threshold of parties to cooperate — typically 2-of-3 or 3-of-5. The key is never reconstructed in a single location.

MPC vs Multi-Sig

Multi-sig requires multiple on-chain signatures, revealing the signing structure publicly and incurring higher gas costs. MPC produces a single signature indistinguishable from a regular transaction — cheaper, more private, and chain-agnostic. MPC works on any blockchain without smart contract support.

MPC vs Hardware Wallets

Hardware wallets store the complete private key on a physical device — a single point of failure. MPC eliminates this by distributing key shares. No physical device to lose, steal, or damage. Signing can happen programmatically without human intervention, enabling automated treasury management.

Enterprise Use Cases

Exchanges use MPC for hot wallet management with automated signing policies. Hedge funds use it for multi-party approval workflows. Custodians use it to meet regulatory requirements without single points of failure. DeFi protocols use it for treasury management with governance controls.

Leading MPC Solutions

Fireblocks, Copper, and Qredo are the leading institutional MPC providers. For custom implementations, libraries like tss-lib (Go) and multi-party-ecdsa (Rust) provide the cryptographic primitives. PumaPulse builds custom MPC wallet infrastructure for exchanges and custodians.
